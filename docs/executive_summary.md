# Executive Summary

**To:** Founding Team  
**Re:** Is the brand building loyalty, or buying transactions?  
**Dataset:** 3,900 customers | D2C Fashion Brand | United States

---

## Business Question

The brand's promotional program spends real margin dollars on discounts and promo codes. The question is whether this investment is creating lasting customer loyalty — or simply conditioning customers to only buy when deals are available.

## Answer

**The brand is not critically promo-dependent, but it is exposed in a specific segment.** 56.8% of estimated revenue ($3.49M) comes from organic buyers who never use discounts. This is healthy. However, 21.2% of revenue ($1.3M) is concentrated in 450 High-Value Promo-Dependent customers who have never completed a purchase without a discount code. If promotions stop, that revenue is directly at risk.

## Top 4 Insights

**1. Two high-spend segments, one key difference.**  
Loyal High-Value (599 customers, 28.6% of revenue) and High-Value Promo-Dependent (450 customers, 21.2% of revenue) both spend ~$76 per order. The only difference: the former never uses discounts (promo score: 4.8/100); the latter always does (promo score: 84.9/100).

**2. The brand's best customers are concentrated in overlooked states.**  
Montana, Alaska, Alabama, Idaho, and Michigan score highest on the five-factor geography opportunity scorecard — above-average spend, customer density, satisfaction, and high-value customer share, with below-average promo dependency. These markets have organic brand pull the brand hasn't deliberately leveraged.

**3. At-Risk Dissatisfied is the most operationally urgent segment.**  
567 medium-to-high value customers (18.9% of revenue) show satisfaction scores below 3.0. Without direct intervention, this group is a leading indicator of future revenue attrition. The window to recover them is now.

**4. The ideal customer profile is specific enough to target.**  
Age 30–57, slightly female, Clothing and Accessories buyer, $76/order, 38 prior purchases, 4.07/5 satisfaction, near-zero promo dependency. These 599 customers drive 28.6% of revenue from 15.4% of the base. Lookalike acquisition using their profile is the highest-ROI acquisition strategy available.

## 3 Recommended Actions

**Action 1: Stop discounting Loyal High-Value customers immediately.**  
These 599 customers never use promos. Any blanket discount reaching them wastes margin. Replace with early access and VIP messaging. Expected impact: immediate margin improvement, zero revenue risk. *Start: Week 1.*

**Action 2: Execute a 12-week staged promo reduction for High-Value Promo-Dependent customers.**  
Phase 1 (Weeks 5–8): Reduce discount depth by 30%. Phase 2 (Weeks 9–12): Replace discounts with free shipping upgrades or product bundles. Phase 3 (Weeks 13–16): Remove for customers who maintained purchase frequency. Track repeat rate weekly; pause if it drops >5%. *Start: Week 5.*

**Action 3: Redirect bargain-hunter promo budget to Organic Mid-Value nurture.**  
The 424 Low-Repeat Bargain Hunters contribute 1.7% of revenue. Stop targeting them with discount campaigns. Move that budget to cross-sell and subscription nudges for the 557 Organic Mid-Value customers who are one step from becoming Loyal High-Value. *Start: Week 8.*

## Expected Impact and Risk

If Actions 1–3 are executed over 24 weeks: estimated margin recovery of $40–80K (discount cost reduction on organic customers) plus 10–15% AOV growth in the Organic Mid-Value segment, adding $80–120K in incremental annual revenue. The main risk is a short-term order frequency dip in the HV Promo-Dependent segment during transition — mitigated by weekly monitoring and a staged rollout with a pause trigger.
